Prediction of depression and suicidality from social media activity

Prediction of depression and suicidality from social media activity using deep neural networks

Princeton University - Senior Thesis

Read here

Abstract: It's estimated that every 13 minutes, at least one American dies by suicide in the United States, making suicide the 2nd leading cause of death in the 15-34 age group (CDC, 2015). Suicide intervention is now one of the top priorities for public health agencies across the world, but unfortunately our screening efforts are still inadequate. In this study we propose a data-driven artificial intelligence approach towards more accurate and higher coverage screening for both suicidal ideation and depression. We trained deep neural networks on Facebook posts from 158 human subjects to distinguish depressed from non-depressed individuals and to distinguish suicidal from non-suicidal individuals, using their Beck Depression Inventory (BDI) and Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS) scores as the ground-truth labels. We achieved state-of-the-art accuracy, precision, and recall rates of 96%, 97%, and 94% respectively on the depression prediction task and accuracy, precision, and recall rates of 92%, 98%, and 83% respectively on the suicidality prediction task. In addition, the deep neural networks outperformed both a trained human psychiatrist as well as other standard machine learning-based classifiers on the same tasks. These results demonstrate that it is possible to achieve more accurate and timely screening for depression and suicidal ideation via deep learning, without the need to first seek out a physician or mental healthcare professional. This significantly lessens the time, effort, and money required to seek help, which is particularly important for low-income and marginalized communities with low access to adequate healthcare.


Seasonal dynamics of bacterial meningitis: a time-series analysis

Global Comparative Study of Bacterial Meningitis Seasonality

L'Institut de Recherche pour le Développement (IRD) - Montpellier, France

I collaborated with Dr. Hélène Broutin from L'Institut de Recherche pour le Développement (IRD) in Montpellier, France and Dr. Nicole Basta from Princeton University to publish a global comparative study of the dynamics of bacterial meningitis in The Lancet Global Health, which I was co-first author of. The goal of my project was to compile a global database of bacterial meningitis incidence time series by scraping public health ministry websites/reports (in addition to manual correspondence) and to analyze the seasonality of this data via wavelet analysis, particularly in countries outside of the African meningitis belt. Whereas distinct seasonality patterns have been thoroughly studied in the African meningitis belt, little is known about its dynamics in other regions of the world, making it difficult to tailor vaccination strategies and effectively control the disease. A stronger understanding of the biological and environmental interactions of the various causative agents of meningitis will help us better understand its virulence and epidemiology.


Reasoning from Radically Incomplete Information

Reasoning from Radically Incomplete Information: The Case of Containers

The Courant Institute of Mathematical Sciences - New York, NY

During my internship at NYU's Courant Institute of Mathematical Science, I researched and published a paper about "common sense logic," wherein humans arrive at logical conclusions despite extremely sparse available information, thereby becoming both more efficient and accurate than computers in information-sparse environments. I programmed a system of mathematical axioms describing first-order spatiotemporal container logic and proved related theorems in SPASS, demonstrating the first steps towards precisely describing the human physical inference process.


Predicting Procedural Risk of TAVR

Predicting Procedural Risk of TAVR

Heffner Biomedical Imaging Laboratory - Columbia University in the City of New York

While volunteering at New York-Presbyterian Hospital and interning at the Heffner Lab, I developed an algorithm for predicting a patient's risk of experiencing severe complications after undergoing a transcatheter aortic-valve replacement (TAVR). The algorithm consisted of several parts - image analysis of CT scans (published here), statistical identification of significant predictor variables, and probabilistic prediction of a patient's outcome using Bayesian machine learning. I wrote the algorithm in a combination of Python and MATLAB and used anonymized CT scans and patient data from New York-Presbyterian Hospital. Ultimately, the algorithm was ~70% accurate.


Probabilistic Analysis of Emptying Time of a Saturated ALOHA System

Probabilistic Analysis of Emptying Time of a Saturated Slotted ALOHA Network

Bell Laboratories - Holmdel, NJ

The slotted ALOHA network protocol, an improvement upon the original ALOHA protocol, was designed to increase the maximum throughput of a network by discretizing time. I researched the properties of slotted ALOHA by designing a probabilistic mathematical model to simulate it, and derived a formula for the amount of time a saturated ALOHA network would require to empty its queued packets, if the system were paused. I tested my formula by simulating a saturated slotted ALOHA network in Mathematica and verified that it realistically modelled such a system.
